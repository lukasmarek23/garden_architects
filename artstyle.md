Top Recommended Styles / Inspirations
Here are some strong directions, drawn from successful children's games, books, and illustrators that use watercolor effectively for nature/garden themes:

## Classic children's picture book watercolor (soft and nostalgic)
Think of the gentle, luminous washes seen in books like Beatrix Potter's garden/animal scenes or Laurence Anholt's "The Magical Garden of Claude Monet" (impressionistic garden vibes with light-filled flowers and paths).
Characteristics: Soft edges, layered transparent colors, subtle gradients, friendly rounded forms.
Why it fits: It evokes wonder and calm—perfect for a peaceful garden defense game. Flowers and trees look inviting rather than sharp/scary. Easy to scale for board tiles/pawns.
    
## Modern board game watercolor (like Floriferous, Meadow, or Village Green)
These games use a painterly, hand-done watercolor aesthetic for nature themes—loose brushstrokes, blended petals/leaves, and a cozy, artsy feel without being overly photorealistic.
Characteristics: Expressive but controlled washes, visible brush texture for charm, harmonious pastel-to-vivid palettes (pinks, lavenders, greens, yellows). Often includes delicate details like tiny insects or dewdrops.
Why it fits: Recent garden-themed games lean this way, and it stands out from generic "cute cartoon" looks while staying kid-friendly.

## Impressionist-inspired loose watercolor (Monet garden vibes)
Light, dappled effects with blooming flowers in soft focus, emphasizing color over fine detail—like a child's-eye view of a magical garden at golden hour.
Characteristics: Blurry edges, glowing highlights, playful color mixing (wet-on-wet technique for organic blends).
Why it fits: Matches your "beautiful watercolor gardens" vision perfectly. It makes the board feel alive and immersive, encouraging kids to imagine tending a real garden.
